Today we began seeing parts of Hadrian's Wall --- exciting! The first was a tall chunk at Hare Hill. This is the tallest part that remains of the Wall, though the top few feet of this piece have actually been rebuilt. Hadrian's Wall was probably about 15 feet originally, but nothing now is that high. Apparently it had stayed almost intact for a thousand years after the Romans left, around 400 AD, because people built houses of timber. But when they began using stone as building material, the Wall was a good source of dressed stones, and the Wall was taken away by the cartload.

 

 

 

 

 

Within a few miles we were seeing more and more of existing Wall. Near the village of Banks we saw our first turret. Along the Wall, spaced one Roman mile apart, there were what we now call milecastles. These housed about 30 men. Between each pair of milecastles were two turrets (observation towers).

 

 

The countryside was beautiful --- rolling hills with trees in bloom, sometimes farming and sometimes livestock grazing. It was very pleasant walking.

 

 

 

 

 

We stopped and looked at a couple more turrets, a third of a Roman mile apart. These were Leahill (51B) and Sandy Sike (51A) turrets. Modern numbering starts at the eastern end of the Wall, giving numbers to milecastles and designating the intervening turrets with A and B. A Roman mile was 1000 double steps, 1620 yards, slightly shorter than our mile.
 

In this area there wasn't much stone readily available, so originally the Wall was built from squares of turf. It was probably about the same height as the stone portion, and quite solid and substantial. A few years later, the Romans rebuilt most of the turf wall in stone, but on this particular stretch, the stone wall was built a few hundred yards to the north of the turf wall. For a short while we were walking beside what remains of the original turf wall. The sheep were lazily sleeping on the top of it.

 

 

 

 

 

After visiting the fort of Birdoswald, we were finally walking beside long segments of Wall. We found (with the help of a guidebook) a centurial stone, one of the stones inserted into the wall when a centurion and his cohort finished an assigned portion of wall-building. This one says "Julius Primus of the VIII Cohort was responsible for building this legionary length." Clearly considerable shorthand was used!

 

 

As we walked beside the Wall, we could see in the distance our route for the next day, up and down the "Nine Nicks of Thirlwall." The central section of Hadrian's Wall follows the top edge of the Whin Sill, a ridge of hard quartz-basalt that extends across northern England to the North Sea. This "sill" rises and falls, often with a rock face on the northern side. Since this helped defensively, the Romans built the wall along the top edge of the sill. The "Nine Nicks of Thirlwall" refer to the (originally) nine gaps in the sill. Modern quarrying has removed several of the rock faces.

 

The Wall along here shows well the two widths that were used. At first the Wall was built ten feet wide. But while construction was in progress, the decision was made to make it eight feet, instead. One conjecture is that this was to save time. Long stretches of base had already been laid at the ten foot specification, so the upper part, completed later, is two feet narrower.

 

 

 

 

 

Half an hour later we came to Poltross Burn Milecastle, the best-preserved milecastle on the Wall. One of its features that's remarked on is the set of steps in the northeast corner. Historians have extrapolated from these steps, extending them up to a presumed parapet walk on top of the Wall, to estimate that the the Wall was probably about 15 feet high.

 
 

 

An hour later, after quite a few fields and footbridges and stiles, we took a short detour to our B&B in Longbyre, ending an eventful day.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

The following day started with a visit to the ruins of 14th century Thirlwall Castle, built mostly with Wall stones. There followed a very enjoyable hour or so in the Roman Army Museum at Carvoran. One video they were showing was a recruitment advertisement for the Roman Army. You get to wear stylish armor, and will become a Roman citizen after 25 years!

 

Now we were at the beginning of the Whin Sill up-and-down part. We passed a disused quarry and the lake that it had formed, and then climed steeply up. At the top, we were walking beside a section of Wall that was higher than any earlier section we'd seen.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

We ate lunch while sitting beside a turret partway up one of the Whin Sill crests, with a view back over the "nick" we had just come up from.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

We were treated to a comedy just before walking through this farm. The lambs were playing games. They tried to chase the rooster, but the rooster turned and faced them and they all ran. Then they tried to frighten the cat on the wall, but the cat growled and they ran. Then they just played by themselves, running and bouncing up in the air. They were hilarious. This should be a movie!

Soon we were at Aesica, or Great Chesters Fort. It's part of a working farm, and the farmer isn't interested in having archaeologists excavating there, so one wanders among mounds with partially exposed stones. The forts along the Wall were built a few years after the Wall itself. Presumably the authorities decided that it was too slow getting troops to the Wall from the existing forts, several miles south.

 

 

Here it looks as if Thann is surreptitiously swiping coins (honest, he wasn't), but the altar is of interest because it's the only altar along the Wall still in place. There are many others in the museums.

 

 

 

 

The little arch was part of the entrance to the underground strongroom, where the soldiers' pay was kept.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

A couple of hours later, before heading off south for our B&B at Once Brewed, we looked ahead to the enticing view of tomorrow's path over the rock face above Crag Lough.  

 

 

The next day started with a cross-country walk, as we found our way south to the fort of Vindolanda. Vindolanda has been owned by a family, now a trust, which is interested in all aspects of the fort --- especially archaelogy and education. There has been excavating there for decades, as the archaeologists dig down and keep finding older forts below. The objects they find have been amazingly well preserved. There are leather boots, and best of all, wooden writing tablets with letters to and from people living there in the first few centuries AD.  
     
There's a large vicus, the name for the towns that grew up around forts. Historians have guesses as to what the various buildings were --- shops, guest houses, as well as homes.  

 

We walked back up to the Wall, and headed east again. Once again we had the pretty view of Crag Lough ahead, and eventually we could also see Castle Nick milecastle below us. We decided that the milecastle would be a good place to have our picnic lunch, so we did just that.

 

 

 

 

 

 

After a couple more hours of very enjoyable walking, including a short stretch where we were allowed to walk actually on top of the Wall, we came to Housesteads Fort. This is one that was built on the line of the Wall, and it has more building foundations than most of the others. The photo on the left is the base of a granary. The granaries in each fort were supposed to hold enough to supply 1000 men for a year - a lot of food. They were always ventilated with air space under the floor, which was supported by many short columns. The picture on the right is of the latrine. This was a communal meeting place, with two rows of seats, and a cistern sending constant running water under the seats.

 

We spent much time happily wandering around Housesteads before setting off for the last couple of miles, over the last of the up-and-down climbs, to our B&B for the night, the Old Repeater Station. Our host there, Les, was great company. He fixed us a good dinner, and we all sat talking (and drinking) till almost midnight.
