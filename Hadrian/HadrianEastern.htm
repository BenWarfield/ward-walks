The first "sight" of the next day was the Temple of Mithras, at Carrawbrugh. Worship of Mithras was a common religion among the Roman soldiers, especially the officers. Several days later when we had finished the walk, we visited the Museum of Antiquities in Newcastle, where there was an exhibit that we all enjoyed. The display is based on this particular site at Carrawbrugh, and it's a partial reconstruction of the temple. We sat on a bench in the darkened reconstruction, as though we were there in the temple itself, and watched shadowy figures in front of us explaining the Mithraic worship service.

 

We soon walked past another fort, Brocolitia, which is entirely un-excavated. There are just lumps and ridges on the ground indicating the presence of ruins under them. Then an hour later we came to "Limestone Corner." This is the renowned spot in the North Ditch where the Roman wall builders seem to have given up on their rock-splitting job. There are even slots still showing on some of the boulders indicating that the workers had tried unsuccessfully to split them by the expansion of wooden wedges inserted and soaked with water (a standard technique). The Whin Sill is made of very hard dolerite rock indeed! So the boulders here are just lying around where the Romans left them, still in the middle of the ditch! We enjoyed exploring among them, looking for signs of the attempted stone-cutting.

 

We walked through more farmland, sometimes fields of bright yellow rapeseed, sometimes grazing fields. There were many sheep, of course, of different varieties. These lambs had strikingly black faces.

 

 

Throughout the walk, we enjoyed the hawthorns with their pretty white blossoms. Here the sheep were enjoying them as a shady place to rest.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

After several hours of pleasant walking we came to Chesters Fort. We started off in the excellent museum. As we entered, we almost tripped over the famous Written Rock, right outside the door. This is a rock from one of the quarries which the wall builders used. It says "PETRA FLAVI(I) CARANTINI", and it's thought that said Flavius was probably the foreman of the gang doing the quarrying.

Inside the small museum there's a collection of hundreds of artifacts from the forts and milecastles and surrounding areas. There are dozens of altars, for example, as well as case after case of pottery, utensils, and clothing. After enjoying the exhibit, about the time we were starting to be overwhelmed by it, we went out to the cafe for coffee.

 

 

 

 

Then we wandered through the ruins of the large fort, seeing barracks blocks and headquarters building and commandant's house, before heading down to the bath-house beside the river. This is Chesters' most famous feature, as its walls are intact to a fairly good height. One walks in by the entrance hall and dressing room, and can wander through the warm room, hot room, cold room, sweating chamber, and hot dry room, among others. Quite impressive!

 

 

 

     
It was still about three miles to our B&B, and we were beginning to flag, but were glad to see the Planetrees section of the Wall. (Planetrees is the name of the farm that it's on.) This section is special for two reasons. The more important historically is that this is exactly the place where the Wall builders (working from east to west) stopped building at the ten-foot width and reduced it to eight feet. But the other reason is that this is the stretch where William Hutton begged workers to stop demolishing the wall. Hutton considered himself the first person to walk the length of the Wall. He did so in 1802, was sure that he would be the last person to walk the whole length (!), and wrote a book about it.  

 

  A mile or so further along we we were happy to see St. Oswald's Church, knowing that our B&B was nearby. Our B&B was St. Oswald's Farm, and the area is called Heavenfield. There was a battle here in 634. Oswald, King of Northumbria, defeated the British. Oswald was Christian and the British were heathen, and this is thought to be a turning point in the conversion of Britain to Christianity. A church was built here, largely from Wall-stone, but the present church was mostly reconstructed in the 18th century.

 

We thoroughly enjoyed our stay at St. Oswald's Farm. Our hosts very kindly fixed us a supper, though they don't generally do this. Then the next day, after breakfast, they told us about the Wall associations there. For one thing, the Wall goes right under the middle of the house! We had read that the house is built with quite a few Wall-stones, and they showed us the Roman stones in their farmhouse wall. And they showed us the extra special stone high up in the front of the house, a centurial stone! (The > sign indicates a centurion.)

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Before leaving the farm, we went back up to see the church. It has a very special feel to it. In the narthex are panels giving the history of the church and information about the other (not many) St. Oswald's churches in the world.  
We walked back past the farm, and then along a particularly well-preserved stretch of the north ditch. This view is looking back (west) toward St. Oswald's farmhouse, seen at the far end of the ditch. The main east-west road here runs exactly on top of Hadrian's Wall, that is, directly to the south (left) of the ditch. This is not an accident. At the time of the Jacobite rebellion in 1747, General George Wade was on the east side of the country, near Newcastle. Bonnie Prince Charlie and his troops advanced south on the west side, near Carlisle. There was no good road for Wade to use. In frustration, he called for the east-west raod to be built. The easiest route was the route that the Romans had taken, at least until the Wall went up onto the Whin Sill. So Wade's men demolished the Wall and used the stones to build a road in the same place. So much for the 20 miles of Wall west of Newcastle!  
     

 

 

Further pleasant walking ensued. After a few miles we made an abrupt turn to the south, for a one-day excursion off the Wall path. This was to the town of Corbridge, mainly to see the Roman fort there, called Corstopitum. Where we turned there was a field with some Belted Galloway cows, with their totally improbable coloring. We stopped in Corbridge for a late lunch and then went to the fort. It has the best preserved granary of any fort, with much of the floor still extant. But what seemed most memorable to us was the striking roller-coaster effect. The stone fort was built on top an of earlier turf fort. The result was that the ground subsides at different rates, depending on what's underneath. It has a very funny, undulating effect!

 

Our B&B was to the south of town, across the Tyne River. We crossed the river on an interesting bridge. The sign on the bridge mentions a flood. There was another bad flood just this past January. The B&B where we stayed had water six feet deep on their ground floor. Now, five months later, the house is finally habitable, after having the whole ground floor gutted and rebuilt. It's almost the only house in the street that's finished; all the other houses are still boarded up. Our hosts suggested a nearby pub for dinner. A young couple had bought the pub only a few weeks before the flood. This was apparently their first week of making dinners again.

 

 

  We spent several hours the next morning in Corbridge. It has seen much history, as a sign above a gate attests:

We left town on a pleasant bridle path, eventually dropping down through a woods, and then suddenly coming up to Aydon Castle. This is more a fortified manor house than a castle, but it was fun to explore.

 

 

 

 

As we understand it, the old movie "Ivanhoe," which starred Elizabeth Taylor, was filmed in part at Aydon. The hangings in the hall above were made by local school children.

There were picnic tables in the castle orchard - just what we wanted. We took a photo of ourselves having our lunch.

 

 

 

 

We walked back up to the Wall, or rather, the route of the Wall. The Wall itself is all underneath General Wade's road, called the Military Road. We spent the night at the B&B next door to Robin Hood's Inn, and enjoyed our dinner there. Then the next day we had our last view of a long section of the Wall, in the town of Heddon-on-the-Wall. There's a curious circular structure in the Wall, a medieval addition. They think it was a kiln or an oven.

 

After this, it was straight into Newcastle. Haway the Toon! The next day we finished the Hadrian's Wall Path off properly, walking to Segedunum, the fort at the eastern end of the Wall. We took one last picture of the four of us, with the last little bit of Wall behind us.

We happened to arrive when some "Romans" were demonstrating marching, and we had fun watching.

 

 

 

 

 

 

We liked Newcastle, especially its bridges. Here are two: the Millennium Bridge is a fantastic structure with two arches that pivot in tandem, allowing boats to pass below, and the Victorian Swing Bridge, hydraulically operated, is where the Romans had their bridge over the Tyne.

 

Thus ended our conquest of Hadrian's Wall!
