We left Hereford the next morning, again crossing the Victoria Bridge. The river must be quite shallow, or maybe this fisherman whom we watched from the bridge was on some sort of waterbar:

We again walked through the park across the river and again enjoyed the view of the cathedral:

We were soon back on familiar terrain, on a path beside the Wye, with those flowers between us and the river.

 

An older gentleman came the other direction, and my guess was that he would know what these flowers were. We asked him, and had a wonderful long chat with him. We told him, when he asked, that we were doing the Wye Valley Walk. He said that he used to walk like that, but he had had a stroke a few years ago. Now he walks only (!) six miles every day, up the river and back. He said that the flowers are impatiens glandulifera, or Himalayan Balsam, and they're familiarly known as policeman's helmet. They are in fact an obnoxious weed and are quite harmful. Their roots are shallow and they cause riverbanks to collapse. Too bad - we thought they were pretty! I took a quick picture of him after he left.

There were several miles of road walking, on a small road with almost no traffic. As it turned out, we had several interesting conversations.

  An ancient (1930s) three-wheeler automobile passed us, and then a few minutes later we caught up with it. The driver had stopped to chat with a couple of friends. Just when we got there the car had to move on because another car came along the road, but the two fellows were cheerful talkers. The taller man gave me some hazlenuts that he had just picked. He opened them for me. Thann talked with the shorter man, and then we both walked with him along the road. He was from Scotland, near Fort William. It was long ago that he left Scotland, but he and his wife go back every year.

 

  Not long after the Scot turned off on a side road we came to a house with a monkey puzzle tree. For some reason - maybe its name - we've always enjoyed these. We stopped to take its picture and a woman came out of the house. She told us a lot about the tree. One fact I remember is that she planted it too near the road, and she has to trim the lower branches frequently.

Once or twice before we've been too tempted and succombed to roadside jams or other goodies. This time we resisted.

An hour or so later we entered the Bulmers orchard. This was a large orchard, but it still was just a small part of Bulmers, as they make tons of cider, including Strongbow and Woodpecker. We appreciated their welcome sign.

 

Nearby St Mary's Church by folklore is the burial place of Owain Glyndwr. Our walk took us through the church's fine old lych gate:

 

  Next came the Monnington Walk. This is a mile-long "avenue", a wide, grassy area, lined with pines and yew trees, planted in the mid-seventeenth century as the approach to Monnington Court. At the far end of this avenue we turned off to find our B&B for the night, Dairy House Farm, part of the estate. They kindly drove us to a pub for dinner, since we didn't fancy walking an extra two or three miles.
