When we left Hay we stopped in back to see John as he worked on his renovation project

and then continued on, again beside the Wye, through more fields, for most of the day.

For a while we were on a sunken path by the river, with the fields well above us. The map showed a standing stone in one of the fields, so we scrambled up the bank and were totally amazed that we had happened to come up at exactly the correct spot! (We don't know anything about the history or the age of this standing stone.)

Llanstephan Bridge was a narrow wooden bridge, surprisingly still used by cars. From far away we could hear occasional cars on the bridge. Thann went out to look at the river:

 
   

It was a shock when a car came roaring onto the far end of the bridge. There definitely wasn't room for a pedestrian beside a car, so Thann ran fast - very fast! - and just made it off the bridge as the car, which hadn't slowed down at all, zoomed past. Whew!

We spent the night in a B&B called "The Old Vicarage", one of several such on our walk. Maybe vicars are no longer provided with a vicarage? As we left the next morning we loooked for a postbox, and were delighted with the one we found:

It was again a rather grey day. We were soon climbing another hill, with occasional views down to the valley:

On top there was pleasant walking for several miles across pastures and on springy turf through the heather:

At one point we came to this hoary finger-post, attesting to the plentitude of moisture in the air:  

As we started down from the hills, the views north of the Wye Valley were again beautiful. I'm not sure whether that spot in the photo was a bird, plane or UFO.

Here it is, zoomed in. You can decide:  
Part of the path down was on a sunken lane. We had heard that one can date the lane by the types of plants in layers on the banks.

    As we came into town we heard the welcome chimes of an ice cream van. We turned a corner - and saw the van pulling off! Thann ran, but sadly couldn't catch the driver's attention.

The town that we were coming into was Builth Wells. It's pronounced "Bilth", and that's anglicized from the Welsh. Bu (cow), gellt (pasture). In the 19th century, "Wells" was added because of the medicinal waters. You'll be happy to hear that we found ice cream at a shop in the town (where we also bought bread and cheese for the next few lunches).

  After Builth Wells we were again beside the Wye. Here's yet another fisherman - the Wye is apparently excellent for salmon and trout fishing.

A couple of hours later we were tired and saw a bench. We were disappointed to see it labeled "Private". We kept walking. After two or three more private benches we said "Phooey to you, whoever you are" and sat down for a snack!

Builth had been about half-way for the day, nine miles before and seven after, but these last seven miles were easy, so that just a few hours later we came to our destination for the night, the New Inn in Newbridge.

Of course neither the town nor the inn is actually "new". In the sixteenth century, this inn replaced an earlier one - hence the "New Inn". We enjoyed talking with the owner, Dave, while waiting for our dinners.
