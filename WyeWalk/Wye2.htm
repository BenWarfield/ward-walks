Wye Valley Walk - Redbrook to Symonds Yat

Intro   Previous   Next

After leaving Redbrook we walked several pleasant miles beside the Wye.

We arrived in Monmouth and stopped in a pub on Agincourt Square. "Agincourt" honors Henry V, who was born in Monmouth. From the window of the pub we could see the two statues, Henry V up on the wall and Charles Rolls with his biplane. Afterwards I took a better picture of them. Rolls, of Rolls-Royce, had a home nearby, and was a biplane enthusiast. In fact, he was the first Briton to die in a flying accident.

 

After an hour or so in Monmouth, we made our way back to the river. It poured rain for a short while as we were leaving town, but then the sun came out brilliantly.

  We came to a church, St Peter's, Dixton, and went in. We were impressed by the list of vicars which begins in 1257 and continues to the present, but then we read that the church is in fact considerably older than that implies. One of the walls has herring-bone masonry, a style used by the Saxons, so the church must have existed in pre-Norman times.
 

As we were walking through a long wooded section by the river, a sign told us that we were entering England. After passing the sign we looked back at it, and sure enough, we had left Wales!

  

An hour later we were at Biblins Bridge, a 200-foot-long suspension bridge across the Wye. It's near a campground and its bounciness is enjoyed by many young campers.

After a short time we arrived in Symonds Yat. We deposited our packs in our guest house and went to a pub, the Saracen's Head. We had an iPod with us on the walk, so we could connect to the internet and check our email whenever we were in a Wi-Fi hotspot. I had looked before the trip for a list of such spots, and the Saracen's Head was on the list.

We checked that the pub served dinner until 9:00, and since it was only 6:00 we figured we had time for a walk around the end of the peninsula that the Wye forms and then back up and over the hill in the middle of the little peninsula. Up on top this hill is "Yat Rock" a (relatively) famous viewpoint. Our choice of route turned out to be not great. Clearly no one had walked that way in a long time, as we had to make our way through head-high growth on marshy paths. After several miles we found a little unmarked path up the side of the hill, and that fortunately turned out to be the path we wanted. We got up to Yat Rock and admired the views in all directions.  
 

A dark cloud had us hurrying onward, back down to Symonds Yat and the Saracen's Head pub. Unfortunately there were several paths going down, all unmarked. After a couple of tries on paths that seemed to turn the wrong direction (Thann's intuition was pretty good), we settled on one and zoomed down it. We really hoped it was right, because twilight had come and gone, and we really wanted to get to the pub in time to eat! Well, it worked - and we very much enjoyed our dinners!
