Wye Valley Walk - Fownhope to Hereford

Intro   Previous   Next

Our walk to Hereford the next day was short, planned that way to give us the afternoon to see the city. An hour after leaving Fownhope we were going through the village of Mordiford. We liked the view back, after we had crossed the bridge over the Lugg.

We read various Edward Elgar references for the next few days, because he lived just up the road from here. His Elegy for Strings, Opus 58, is said to have been inspired by the view from this bridge. We walked for almost a mile along the flood banks of the Lugg and came to the village of Hampton Bishop. We took a short detour to visit its church, and we were very glad we did. It's an interesting church, mostly 12th century, built of local sandstone, with an unusual medieval topping on its Norman tower.

There was a table inside with used books for sale. We happily reduced our backpack weights by a pound by donating the books we had brought to read on the plane!

The door is typical Norman, with the chevron pattern in the carvings.

     The sign, however, is not Norman! I hope visitors heed their request and keep the birds out!

Not much later we were crossing a wheat field to return to our riverside walking.

Our usual riverside flower greeted us.

 

We had been walking upstream beside the Wye, and it had been navigable for only the first few days of our walk. We were thus startled to see a barge. Hmmmm......

There was a surprisingly long walk on city streets through the outskirts of Hereford, but we finally arrived at our B&B. We were too early and the owner was busy cleaning our room, but she kindly made tea for us and we relaxed comfortably with tea and cookies in her lounge. We then set off to explore Hereford. We quickly spotted an ice cream van, much to Thann's delight.

Our first objective was to find a place to get some prints of pictures that I had taken. We had been forced to buy a new camera on the first day of the walk when my camera stopped functioning, and we wanted to be sure that the new camera was in fact working. The Boots in Hereford had the processing capabilities we needed, and the pictures looked all right, so we proceeded on to the sightseeing. We ate our sandwiches in a park, wandered around, and finally visited the cathedral.

 

  We had to search a bit for the statue of Sir Edward Elgar leaning on his bicycle. There's construction going on around the cathedral, and Elgar was hidden away behind fences.

We were struck by the grass tennis courts in the park across the river.

 

We had crossed and recrossed the Wye several times using the interesting bridges. The Old Bridge was built in 1490, replacing one from 1125.

 

The Victoria Bridge, a pedestrian bridge, was opened in 1898 in commemoration of Queen Victoria's Diamond Jubilee the year before.

We found a Tesco and bought lunch foods for the next few days, as well as salads for dinner. We sat in the park across the river and ate while sitting on a comfortable bench and admiring the cathedral. We enjoyed our afternoon in Hereford!
