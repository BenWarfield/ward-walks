Wye Valley Walk - Symonds Yat to Fownhope

Intro   Previous   Next

 

  We left Symonds Yat by again going up and over the hill, past Yat Rock. Down on the other side we followed the river around another huge loop. At just the right time we came to a bench beside the church at Welsh Bicknor and we stopped for a mid-morning snack. Then back to riverside walking, sometimes through patches of an almost ubiquitous flower. (We learned later what it is.)

 

 

We made a detour of a couple of miles to go up to Goodrich Castle. Its history begins in the 12th century, and it was finally ruined by Oliver Cromwell's troops in the 1640s, during the Civil War.

 

It was beseiged by a mortar named "Roaring Meg" which is now in the castle courtyard.

Roaring Meg herself:

   We ate our sandwiches at a picnic table by the cafe and had a bottle of their cider, named Roaring Meg!

The afternoon walking was up and down hills away from the Wye. Maybe there aren't rights of way beside the river. We arrived finally in Ross-on-Wye, our stopping place, back beside the river.

 

We stayed in a guest house with lots of bay windows. Here's Thann putting on his boots the next morning:

   We then wandered around for a while in Ross, a pleasant market town. It has a sandstone market hall:

 

After leaving Ross we were back down by the river again for a few miles. The Wye Valley Walk is marked by signs with a leaping salmon.

  The frequency of the signs varies tremendously; in some areas there would be a sign at most path choices; in other areas there would be no signs for miles. We had maps that generally (but not always) kept us going in the right direction. Sometimes the arrows puzzled us, but in this case it was easy - we knew we were supposed to turn right, away from the river.  

There were then several miles of fields:

It was warm, and the cows were cooling off in the shade of the trees.

 

Across the river was Foy Church. Its original structure dates to 1050, though most of the current building is a few centuries newer. The Normans didn't recognize Celtic saints, and thus the church's name was changed from St Ffwy to St Foi.

    

There were frequently mute swans beside or in the river. Swans aren't as friendly as they look - we've been snapped at in the past.

I'm not sure what breed of cattle these are, with their long horns, but we were coming close to Hereford and were looking forward to seeing Hereford cows.

After a little road-walking, down past a blacksmith's forge (the red building),

we were back in fields. That bird may be a red kite, a bird of prey that used to be an endangered species. Now they're fairly commonly seen in this area.

We spent the night in a black-and-white in the small town of Fownhope. We liked this B&B, with its 15th century low doorways and interesting corners.
