Wye Valley Walk - Newbridge to Llangurig

Intro   Previous   Next

This was going to be a short walk today, so we set breakfast for 8:30 and got some extra sleep. When we went downstairs the doors to the dining room were locked. We were puzzling about what to do when Dave appeared. He said that he always wakes up at 7:00, but this morning he slept till 8:10. His wife was away for a day, picking up their son who was in Bath. Dave was clearly having problems managing without her.

The morning's walking again included a hill to climb. We ate lunch in a pleasant spot, leaning against a stone wall, looking at a pretty heather-covered hillside, and shooing off sheep that were interested in our food.

After a long, leisurely lunch we started down the hill.

Half an hour later we were down at river level, crossing another bouncy suspension bridge across another tributary of the Wye.

Less than an hour later we were approaching Rhayader, down through a churchyard.

 

  Rhayader (Rhaeadr Gwy, or Waterfall on the Wye) is a historic market town. It's small, with a population under 3,000, but it's a population center for this rural area. Rhayader is the first town on the Wye, counting from the source of the Wye.

We walked up the main shopping street toward the town clock.

 

We marveled at the ability of the cars and trucks to maneuver around the clock tower and each other, seeming to know what they were doing. Rhayader has always been a natural crossroads, and now the clock tower is at the intersection of two of mid-Wales's busiest roads. A bypass has been under discussion for years.

We were there at 4:15, as one can see. At the corner by the clock was the inviting "Old Swan Tea Shop", so in we popped for tea and scones.

Our hotel was on the farther corner by the clock, and we enjoyed our dinner there, eating while looking out the window at the traffic display.

  

The next morning started with several miles of road-walking, mostly climbing a hill, but then came a very enjoyable stretch. We were in fields, and then we descended on a pleasant path with springy turf underfoot and great views ahead.

We came to Gilfach Nature Reserve and farm. There was a Visitor Centre with exhibits. A friendly volunteer offered to make us tea, so we had a fine break. There has been a farm here for many centuries, and traditional methods of farming have always been used. There are thus many varieties of plants and wildlife that have disappeared elsewhere. After our tea we went into the 15th century longhouse, across the farmyard.

 

We finally left the farm, walking in the valley of the Marteg, a small stream.

The path that we were on a bit later was the Monks' Trod, used by Cistercian monks, before Henry VIII demolished the monasteries, going between the monasteries of Strata Florida and Abbey Cwm Hir. Gerald of Wales, in the 12th century, would have walked this path.

 

When we dropped down and once again crossed the Wye, it was decidedly a smaller river.

The sun came out brilliantly as we walked up the hillside.

In the afternoon we were back by the river.

We were fascinated by the sight of a herd of sheep charging down a hillside. I took a picture, but then had to change the camera's memory card. By the time I got the job done, the sheep were mostly down, and we saw the reason for their hurry - there was a vehicle of some sort behind them.

We spent the night in Llangurig at another "Old Vicarage", yet another enjoyable B&B. It was one of those lovely situations where we're met at the door with a friendly "I'll show you your room, and when you come down there will be tea and cakes for you in the lounge." What a life!

We had dinner at the Blue Bell Inn (at the right), and looked at the church (at the left), dedicated to Saint Curig. The "Llan" at the beginning of many Welsh town names means an enclosure, or a church, or a parish surrounding a church, or ... The web site for the town of Betws-y-Coed says (very explicitly) "There are over 430 places in Wales that start with 'Llan'. A 'Llan' was a piece of land enclosed by a wooden fence within which would be a church and a group of Christians. Each 'Llan' was established around the 6th century by a Celtic saint after whom the site was named."
