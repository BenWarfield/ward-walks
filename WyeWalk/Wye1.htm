Wye Valley Walk - Chepstow to Redbrook

At the start of the walk is a map, of a sort that we saw a good many times over the next two weeks. The map shows several miles of the path (red and yellow) and landmarks in the area. You can see the "You are here" in Chepstow, and the path for the first six or seven miles of the day, up past Tintern. The spots marked as viewpoints (with rays) were well above the river - the map doesn't show elevation changes!

The walk begins on a path up the hill behind Chepstow Castle. The castle is the oldest surviving stone castle in Britain, built soon after the conquest in 1066.

 

For several miles the walk is through the Piercefield Estate, well above the Wye. The walks in the estate were designed in the 18th century, when tourism came to this area.There are follies and viewpoints along the way:

It started raining before we reached Tintern. In fact, the last half mile down (really down) to Tintern was on a very slippery rocky path that had once been a drovers' route. Pity the poor cattle! We did no more than glance at Tintern Abbey as we passed, remembering many pleasant visits there. We knew that there was a cafe in the Tintern Old Station set of restored old railway cars, and we hoped to warm up and dry off while drinking a pot of tea. Unfortunately many people had that idea and the tables were full. We kept walking and the rain stopped not too much later. We ate our sandwiches and apples while happily sitting on a bench and drying off.

Then it was back uphill again, up above the river. For reward there were beautiful views down:

It was hours later when we once again descended to the river level. We then walked beside the Wye for several miles.

It was almost 7pm when we finally got to Redbrook, after a long day. There we crossed the river on a pedestrian bridge which was formerly a railway bridge.

We were very happy when we looked at the village and spotted the Blue Bell Inn, our place for the night. It's the white building at the right:

 

We crossed the bridge, looked back under it, and found with glee the "Wolfman Jack" inscription on a girder. We had enjoyed it many years ago when we went through Redbrook on the Offa's Dyke Path.

We liked the Blue Bell Inn, we enjoyed our dinners, and we sank exhaused into bed!
