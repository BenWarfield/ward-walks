Borders Walk

Jedburgh to Morebattle to Kirk Yetholm

Intro   Previous   Next

Since we had made a detour off St Cuthbert's Way to spend the night (again) in Jedburgh, we started the day with a couple of miles returning to the route. First, as we left town, we retraced our steps along the Jed Water, across from the cliff-face of Old Red Sandstone.

Further along, we could look to our left, over the field of rape seed, and still see the Eildon Hills as well as the Waterloo Monument.

We were glad to walk again through a row of gorgeous horse chestnut trees. When we had come through six days before, the wind had been fierce, and we hadn't been surprised to find a freshly-fallen tree limb across the path. All was calm this morning.

We arrived back at Dere Street, for the last bit of it that we would be walking. Interesting to contemplate that St. Cuthbert, back in the 7th century, would likely have walked along this very route. But we soon turned off, for some flowery edge-of-woodland walking. The forget-me-nots and the red campion are a great combination.

 

Half an hour later we came down through another woods to a footbridge crossing Oxnam Water, with more Old Red Sandstone cliffs. Here we're looking back down at it, pausing as we climbed up (and up and up) a hill.

Several hours later we were hungry, but it was hard finding a place to sit. The woods were all wet, with no inviting surfaces. But out of the woods, the wind had picked up and made stopping unappealing. This wall beside a rape-seed field cut the wind,

and when we crossed a stile at a wall corner, we found a lovely spot!

After lunch, over another hill, we had our first view of the Cheviots, the mountain range that we would be skirting during the next day or so.

Cessford Castle (visible in the picture above as a small red rectangle) was our next landmark. It looked interesting, as we approached it:

It was a stronghold of the Ker family, a family of Border Reivers in the Jedburgh area. But it turned out that one can't go into the building, as it's unsafe.

  

The Ker family was not a peace-loving bunch of people. Spelled sometimes Ker, sometimes Kerr, the two branches of the family were in a perpetual deadly feud. The Cessford branch fought for James VI, while the other branch supported Mary, Queen of Scots.

An hour later we were in Morebattle, our stop for the night. The origins of the name have nothing to do with war - it's from "mere", meaning lake, and "botl", an Anglian word for hamlet. What a pretty village! We stayed at the Templehall Inn, a "family run village inn, owned and run by a local couple". The two rooms we stayed in were half the hotel rooms - it wasn't enormous. We thoroughly enjoyed both the inn and the village.

The next morning we stopped in the village shop for lunch supplies. The village people were so cheerful and friendly!

 

Then it was off to climb Wideopen Hill. After a bit of roadwalking, we turned and crossed Kale Water by a little footbridge, heading into the fields. And the climb started.

  

After some stiles, and endless good views, the last bit to the top was beside a wall.

This was a short day, and we were in Kirk Yetholm by early afternoon. Kirk Yetholm is known as the ending point of the Pennine Way. We were amused by the decoration in front of one of the village cottages - boots, presumably of walkers who had completed the PW and discarded their worn out footwear!

 

Our B&B was just across the green from the Border Hotel, the official end of the Pennine Way.

   We celebrated the memory of our accomplishment back in 2002 by having dinner, again, at the Border Hotel. The dinners were all quite good - mine was a pancake stuffed with wild mushrooms and spinach, with a light cheese sauce - yum!
