Borders Walk

Hawick to Selkirk to Melrose

The next day started with several miles of uphill walking on an almost unused road. No one likes road walking, but this was pleasant. There was lots of yellow gorse to brighten the scene.

We finally turned onto a farm road and were soon in a large field with dozens and dozens of ewes with their lambs. We had a thoroughly delightful half hour walking through the field.

 

Most sheep have two lambs, so the mother-with-two-lambs sets were all around - as were nursing lambs.
 

An hour later we came to a golf course, which we walked through and beside for many holes. For several holes we walked just behind a group of cheerful young men, not wanting to get in the way of their shots. At the end of the course, our route headed back uphill.

 

There was pleasant walking through farms, all afternoon. As we were going through a field near Selkirk, our directions said to pass an old stone dovecote, so we gathered that that's what this building was:  

 

    We were soon in Selkirk. Sir Walter Scott was county sheriff here from 1803 to 1832, and there's a statue of him in the Market Square "Erected in August 1839 in proud and affectionate remembrance".

There are the ruins of an old church, called "Kirk o' the Forest", in the place where William Wallace was proclaimed "Guardian of Scotland" in 1298.

  

We found a Spar Market in Selkirk and bought lunch materials for the next few days, as well as salads for dinner that evening. Our B&B was well out of town, and we figured we wouldn't want to walk back to town after getting there. It was in fact an excellent, elegant B&B. Our hostess greeted us with the offer of a pot of tea and shortbread cookies, which we happily accepted. She chatted with us, and we admired the scene just beyond the French doors in her sitting room - a hillside with a waterfall.

The next morning started with a pretty walk through Selkirk Common. Soon thereafter we began seeing horse riders. Then we realized that this was an organized event, as the riders all had on similar vests.

We asked a rider and learned that it was an endurance and navigational skills competition. We had read about many riding events in the Selkirk and Hawick area. There are the "Common Ridings" annually, commemorating historical happenings in the Borders, but we weren't there at the right time for those.

Around lunch-time we came to Abbotsford, Sir Walter Scott's home. One approaches it down a path from the hillside above. Scott spent many years having it built, in this rather fanciful medieval style. We didn't actually pay to see the house itself, but there was a little cafe, so we enjoyed tea and sandwiches instead!

 

For a couple of days we had been seeing the Eildon Hills in the distance. These are three volcanic hills visible for many miles in all directions. They are behind Melrose, where we were to spend the night, and during the day they kept growing in height. There are two in most pictures; the third peak is usually hidden.

We were in Melrose early enough to leave our packs at the B&B before going to the abbey. Melrose was founded by David I, in 1136, though its ruins are all from a rebuilding after it was heavily damaged by the English in the 14th century.

We climbed the bell tower - but obeyed the admonition not to go up the final set of steps.

 

We chose one of the multitudinous pubs in town for dinner and celebrated having finished the Borders Abbeys Way. Actually, we were omitting the last leg of it, from Melrose to Kelso, where we had started. We wanted to start the St. Cuthbert's Way, which begins in Melrose. We went to bed, primed to start St. Cuthbert's Way by climbing the Eildon Hills the next morning.
