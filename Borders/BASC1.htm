Borders Walk

Kelso to Jedburgh to Hawick

Intro     Next

We spent our first night in Kelso. and the next morning we started out by going to the abbey ruins. This was once a great abbey, with its construction begun in 1128 and extending some 75 years. It was damaged during the 1300s during wars with England, and was repaired. But it was attacked again repeatedly by the English in the 1540s. In 1545 the abbey was almost completely destroyed, never to be restored.

 

 

We left Kelso, crossing the Tweed over an interesting bridge. John Rennie, who designed it, later built Waterloo Bridge in London as a copy. When the Waterloo Bridge was demolished, the lamps were brought to the Kelso Bridge.

   From the bridge one looks upstream to the place where the Teviot flows into the Tweed. This is called "Junction Pool", and it's apparently a mecca for salmon fishermen. You can just see a boat. It's called a coble, and the coble boatmen hold the boat steady at the best places for the fisherman in the boat to catch a salmon. The mansion is Floors Castle, said to be the largest inhabited castle in Scotland. It's in fact not a fortified castle, but an 18th century country home, the home of the Duke of Roxburghe.

We were soon walking beside the River Teviot, beginning what turned out to be a frequently recurring scene - a pretty and pleasant path by a river, with wildflowers in profusion.

We passed the scant ruins of Roxburgh Castle on the hillside above us. It was built in the 1120s by King David I, who founded most of the abbeys we visited. This castle changed ownership between the Scots and the English many times over the centuries. The castle was taken by the English during the Rough Wooing in the 1540s and then demolished as part of a peace treaty in 1551.

      

 

After passing the ruins of a 16th century tower, a stronghold of the locally prominent Ker family, the route went under the Roxburgh Viaduct, part of one of the 19th century, but now disused, railway lines.  

 

 

  After another mile or so of pleasant walking beside the Teviot, the way took us up the railway embankment and onto the path of the former railway. The next three miles were along the old railway line.

 

An hour later we were back down, crossing the river and sitting to eat lunch. We were startled and amused when we happened to turn around and see the strange and inquisitive sheep that had silently come up behind us!  

Soon after lunch we had our first view of the Waterloo Monument. This monument was in sight for us on many days throughout our walk, as we circled around it, left the area, and then returned when walking St. Cuthbert's Way.

We were soon on another stretch of the old railway, now bordered by hawthorn trees.

 

Then we came to "Dere Street". Dere Street was the Roman road that led from York to the Firth of Forth. For much of its length it's now under modern roads, including the A1. But in this area there are miles of Dere Street still walkable. As with almost all Roman roads, it's essentially straight.  

We arrived at our destination for the night, Jedburgh, early enough to spend some time at the abbey. This was also founded by King David I in the early 1100s, and it too was demolished during the Rough Wooing in the 1450s.

We love this abbey, and happily wandered around it for a good while.

 

One can go up spiral steps in the west wall and arrive at a view down the nave. What a great way to end our first day of walking!

After a good dinner and an enjoyable B&B stay, we set off the next morning. We were soon on a lane with yet more colorful flowers. In addition to the hawthorn, there was a mixture of gorse and Scotch broom, both cheerfully bright yellow.

But an hour later, after we had climbed steadily up onto an open hilltop, storm clouds began developing. In fact, we hastily put on our rain jackets when hail started to pummel us. We had climbed about 1000 feet, on Black Law. "Law" is a Scots word for hill.

The rain stopped and started, and our coats went off and on. A couple of hours later we descended to the town of Denholm.

We stopped in a small cafe and dried off while eating soup and sandwiches for lunch. But by the time we left, the rain was pouring down. We put on our full supply of rain gear and set off. It rained most of the afternoon, as we walked along the pretty riverside path to Hawick. Hence no more pictures for this second day!
