The first day of our St. Cuthbert's Way began with a climb up the Eildon Hills, to the col, or gap, between two of them. As we climbed we frequently stopped to look back at Melrose. Here's an early view back, with the abbey still visible:

We had seen the hillside gorse from afar, so we knew we'd eventually be walking through it.

 
     
 

Down toward Melrose, from farther up the hill:

An hour or so later we had descended the other side of the Eildons, had stopped for snacks in the village of Bowden, and were walking beside Bowden Burn. Here, as was often the case, we were walking in a thick growth of wild garlic. It's fortunate we all like garlic, because the air is filled with its scent.

The view back toward the Eildon hills a little later now showed the three peaks:

 

A few miles later we were at the final one of our four great abbey ruins, Dryburgh Abbey. It too was established under the influence of David I, in the 12th century. We spent a long time wandering around, admiring the evocative old sandstone buildings.

  
     
 

We happily found a well-placed picnic table for our lunch:

After visiting the abbey we had a pleasant walk beside the River Tweed, the path again thick with wild garlic.

We found our B&B, a mile on the far side of St. Boswell's, and relaxed for a while before going back into town for dinner. We had very good meals at the Buccleuch Arms Hotel (that's pronounced "buh-kloo"), a friendly country inn.

The B&B was a rather grand house set in lovely grounds, and I particularly liked the walled garden as we walked down her drive the next morning. It reminded me of a childhood favorite book, "The Secret Garden". What was behind the wall?

We stopped (for a good while) at a good book shop in St. Boswell's - someone at the B&B in Melrose had told us about it. When we finally left town, we almost immediately were up above the Tweed again, again with the Eildon Hills in sight. We stood and talked for a while with a man who was leaning on a fence, enjoying the view. He said he loves it, and he stops there every day on his walk.

 

 

Soon we were back down beside the river, looking up flowers in the book and watching fishermen.

  

We had lots of fun a little farther along, where there was a bank filled with sand martin holes. The martins were zipping in and out in great numbers and at great speeds. We kept trying to get a picture of them - but with no luck. You'll have to take my word for it, there really were dozens of them!

 

Downstream a little more was the "Crystal Well". This was an interesting scheme devised by the homeowners on top the hill above the river, in the 1800s. They built a machine to pump water from a spring up to their house. The pump, in one of the two upper rooms, was worked by donkeys, going round and round. The spring is in the lower room.

  

 

An hour later we were back on Dere Street, the Roman road. This time we would be on it for a three-mile stretch, still straight, of course. This is part of the Roman Heritage Way, and that route is appropriately marked by a Roman helmet. Our St Cuthbert's Way was signed with a cross.

  

We crossed a very bouncy suspension bridge across the River Teviot,

walked beside the Teviot for a while, and an hour or so later were back in Jedburgh for the night. We had liked our B&B when we stayed in Jedburgh five days earlier, and we all felt like old friends when our host and hostess greeted us.
