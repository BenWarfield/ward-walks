Borders Walk

Kirk Yetholm to Wooler

Intro   Previous   Next

 

  For our first few miles after leaving Kirk Yetholm, St.Cuthbert's Way accompanied the Pennine Way. These were the last few miles of the Pennine Way, and late afternoon would have seen tired bodies dragging themselves up the path.

In fact, the fingerpost also shows that we were on the E2, a 3030-mile route from Galway in Ireland to Nice in France. The part in Ireland hasn't been completed yet, so the walk now starts in Stranraer, where the ferry from Ireland arrives in Scotland. The E2 follows the Southern Upland Way eastward across Scotland, and in Melrose it picks up St. Cuthbert's Way. We had been on the E2 since Melrose, but I don't recall seeing any other markers for it.

The sky was overcast, and when we heard a rumble we assumed it was thunder. But it didn't sound quite right. Then after another rumble we realized the sound came from the Otterburn military training base in the south Cheviots. The rumbles continued for hours.

We knew that we'd be turning left off the Pennine Way, and we spotted the fingerpost ahead. You may just see it, with our fainter path going to the left.

We soon arrived at the border fence. We had walked beside the border fence for most of a day when we did the Pennine Way in 2002. It was fun seeing it again, as we crossed it into England. (I hit some wrong button on my camera, so the picture is out of whack.)  

 

At first England looked just like Scotland. But then we entered the woods on the right. Our book said "Aim for the front bottom corner of a plantation". ("Plantation" in Britain means an area with planted trees.) In the plantation, "Where you are uncertain, look out for the tree trunks with their lower branches lopped off." We thought this an odd statement, until we entered the exceedingly dark woods.  

The book's advice turned out to be invaluable!

We emerged from the plantation at a stile decorated with a feather. Some walker ahead of us had put feathers along the way, and we enjoyed seeing them.

   Out into a sheep field - don't you think this ewe was saying "How did you escape? No one else has ever done it!".

After a few hours we had left the wild uplands of the Cheviots and were back in tamer (though still sparsely inhabited) farming country.

But then after another mile or so we were in a large expanse of heather moorland. Even though the heather wasn't in bloom, it was all pretty, with patches of cotton grass mixed in.

We were in the heather moorland, up and down hills, for three or four miles of easy, pleasant walking.

We left the moorland suddenly at a gate into grassland, and almost simultaneously we saw a rainbow (yes, it had been raining) and our town for the night, Wooler.

We still had about three miles to go, but at least we had sighted the town!
