Borders Walk

Wooler to Lindisfarne

Intro   Previous    

 

When we were leaving Wooler we passed a school with impressive lion carvings at its gates. We read that the school was on the site of a World War II prison camp, and that these lions had been carved by Italian prisoners of war.  

As we climbed up onto Weetwood Moor, the views back toward Wooler and the Cheviots beyond were excellent.

 

Weetwood Moor has many prehistoric remains, all somewhat off the path. We decided to try to find a "cup-and-ring" carving that according to our book was only about 300 yards off route. After maybe 15 minutes of floundering through heather, we surprised ourselves by finding it. No one knows for sure the reasons for these stone-age markings, but they must have had some religious significance.  

Half an hour later we had crossed the moor and started down toward the River Till and the old Weetwood Bridge. This bridge was used by the English troops in 1513, on their way to the Battle of Flodden.

The bridge was restored in the 18th century and again in the 21st. It's usable, but only if you hold your breath and haven't eaten too many sticky toffee puddings!

  

 

  On our walks we occasionally pass pillboxes, squat concrete forts that were put up in 1940 in preparation for the almost inevitable German invasion. There were some 28,000 of them, unused thanks to the RAF pilots.

 

After some more walking on country lanes, we were to turn off through a field. We looked at the gate into the field and were not happy at what we saw.

These bovines did not look friendly and welcoming. We think they were young bullocks. They seemed to be daring us to come into their field.

We took the dare, and most of them grudgingly moved enough to let us by. But one young fellow kept charging at us, stopping just short of us, snorting and shaking his head, flinging spittle. We walked on, as calmly and steadily as we could manage. He finally stopped, but that was one of our more uncomfortable few minutes.

  

The next objective was St. Cuthbert's Cave. This was, it's said, the spot where monks from Lindisfarne rested for the first night when they were fleeing from the Vikings. They were carrying the coffin with St. Cuthbert's remains. Unbelievably, they carried it from place to place for seven years.

After climbing over the hill behind the cave, we had our exciting first glimpse of the end of our walk - the island of Lindisfarne. There's a castle on a little hill on the island, and that's what's visible from far away. But you do have to look closely:

 

That was still a day away for us. It was another couple of hours of walking to the village of Fenwick, where we were to spend the night. Sometimes Lindisfarne was in view, coming ever closer.  

 

  The following day we had just a couple of miles to walk before reaching the causeway to Lindisfarne. One interesting event was the crossing of the East Coast Main Line. The trains pass at 125 mph, and one is supposed to telephone a signalman to ask permission to cross. A couple coming toward us, after walking over the tracks, told us that they hadn't managed to make the phone on their side work.While we were talking, a train whizzed past, and the couple looked a little shaken. We did as we were instructed, lifting the receiver in a box and pushing a button. We were told that we could proceed, which we did.

There was another World War II reminder that we encountered just before reaching the North Sea. We were walking parallel to the edge of the sea through giant concrete anti-tank blocks, part of the coastal defence.

Lindisfarne is an island part of each day, when the causeway is under water. One has to check the tide tables and plan accordingly. We had checked long before, and had in fact decided to stay in Fenwick on that basis. One could walk on the side of the causeway. But the other possibility, which we chose, was to walk across the sands as St. Cuthbert would have done. It was 2 1/2 miles that way, somewhat shorter than the causeway. It was exciting! There was sometimes easy sand, sometimes shell-covered sand, sometimes water-covered (a couple of inches) sand, and sometimes mud. There was a squelchy weed-covered deep stream to cross. The wind was blowing very hard, and it was cold, so our bare feet were quickly numb. A storm blew in, and we were drenched in a matter of seconds. But we were so glad that we had finished the walk that way!
