Anglesey Coastal Path

April/May 2015

Penmon to Cemaes

Previous Next Intro


After four or five miles of mostly farmland, and the hilltop of Brydd Arthur, the path headed down a lane toward Red Wharf Bay.

Red Wharf Bay is long.  It was five or six miles of walking around the bay, sometimes on the sand, sometimes up above, sometimes through dunes, and sometimes through marshy areas.  In the photo below, we were on a sea wall for a quarter-mile or so.

We crossed several stone bridges:

and after a short flower-filled woods path we emerged at the Ship Inn.  We knew it was coming, and we happily stopped for a beer and a picnic lunch.

The afternoon brought more pleasant walking, sometimes above the water

and sometimes on the shore-line.

 

We relaxed in our B&B in Moelfre, enjoying the view out of our bedroom window:

 

Someone we had chatted with earlier in the afternoon, when we had said that we were going to Moelfre, told us about the lifeboat launch at the Moelfre RNLI station at 6:30.  This was to be a special occasion, as the lifeboat station has been recently rebuilt, and it was still in the testing phase.  We got there at 6:30, just in time to see the boat zoom down the slipway.

Back here at home, I looked on the web for more information on this launching.  I was amazed to find some pictures taken by a professional photographer, Brian Tuohey, who took his pictures at exactly the same time as I.  And you can see the two of us!

To show a rather typical dinner, here we are eating fish and chips at a pub in Moelfre.

Dic Evans was a heroic coxswain of the Moefre Lifeboat, and this excellent statue at the RNLI station was unveiled by Prince Charles, in 2004.

We talked with our B&B hostess about the RNLI.  Her husband is on the crew, and we asked how frequently he goes out.  There are several "shouts" a day on summer weekends.

The next day as we were leaving town, we walked for a bit with an older woman, and we chatted.  It turned out her brother had been coxswain of the lifeboat after Dic Evans.

An hour later, we came to another of the four outdoor paintings, this one at Lligwy Beach.

As we walked around the bay, the flowers seemed particularly cheerful and beautiful.

For two or three miles we were walking around the Dulas Estuary, mostly though farms.  And of course through sheep.

A few miles along the coast brought us to Point Lynas.  Here our general direction changed.  We had been walking mostly northwest.  Now we began walking generally west, across the north side of the island.  For centuries this point has been a treacherous spot for ships heading toward Liverpool.  A beacon was built here in 1779, and this lighthouse dates from 1835.  Here's a look back toward the lighthouse on Point Lynas, after we had passed it.

A couple of miles along, we happily stopped for coffee (and warming up) in a cafe in Amlwch.  Another four miles of up and down coastal walking brought us to Porth Wen.  This is a bay with abandoned brick works, which in times past produced silica bricks from quartzite quarried nearby.

Then followed another several miles of coastal walking, almost constantly up or down - though here was a level section where I took a couple of pictures.

Then onward to spend the night in the attractive village of Cemaes.
